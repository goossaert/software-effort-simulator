# Five-point tail-percentile summary (P10–P90) with probability-of-exceedance against capacity

For every **Scenario** in a **Run** the simulator reports six summary statistics — P10, P25, P50, P75, P90, and the arithmetic mean — alongside the *probability of exceedance*, `P(effort > capacity)`, computed against the user-configured **Capacity**. The six points and the exceedance probability together are the **Stats** tuple consumed by the org-level table (`renderStatsTableInto`, `index.html:2357`), the per-team tables (feature [0011](../../backtracked-features.md#0011)), and the per-marker rows in feature [0017](../../backtracked-features.md#0017). The Stats tuple is produced by `computeStats` (`index.html:2048`) from the sorted `Float64Array` returned by `runScenario` ([feature 0003](../plans/0003-monte-carlo-simulation-engine.md)); the engine guarantees sortedness, this layer relies on it.

We chose the five-percentile fence **P10 / P25 / P50 / P75 / P90** over a three-number summary (P5 / P50 / P95), over Tukey's five-number summary (min / Q1 / median / Q3 / max), and over a single mean ± stddev because the questions users arrive at the table with are *"how likely are we to overrun, and by how much in the realistic-but-not-extreme case"*. P10 and P90 frame the *plausible* range — the band the team should plan against — while P25, P50, and P75 trace the *typical* shape, so the reader can see the distribution's skew without leaving the table for the chart. The arithmetic mean is included as a sanity check, not a redundant centre measure: a mean noticeably above P50 is the simplest visual cue that the distribution's right tail is doing the work, which is the regime the **Lognormal effort distribution** ([ADR-0007](./0007-lognormal-effort-distribution.md)) makes most informative. We deliberately *did not* include P95 or P99 because (a) at the default 10,000 **Iterations** P99 is a low-double-digit count of samples and Monte Carlo noise dominates — the cell would change visibly Run-to-Run without the underlying plan changing, which would erode the table's authority — and (b) those points are exactly the territory custom **Markers** ([feature 0017](../../backtracked-features.md#0017)) are designed for: a team that cares about P95 can add a marker at the PM value they care about, and the stats table grows a per-marker `P(effort > value)` row using the same risk-tier colouring as the capacity row ([ADR-0013](./0013-three-tier-risk-colouring.md)). Tukey's min / max were rejected for the same Monte-Carlo-noise reason and because the **Outlier clip** ([ADR-0011](./0011-overlapping-histograms-shared-bins.md)) already trims the visual upper end; reporting an unclipped maximum in the table would contradict the chart and confuse the reader.

We chose **probability of exceedance** — `P(effort > capacity)` as the *fraction* of **Iterations** whose total effort strictly exceeded **Capacity** — over (a) reporting capacity as a percentile rank ("Capacity sits at P67"), (b) reporting expected overage (`E[max(effort − capacity, 0)]`), and (c) reporting a gap at a fixed percentile (`P90 − capacity`). The fraction form is the most direct answer to the question the row exists to answer ("if we commit to this plan, how often does effort exceed the budget?"), maps one-to-one onto natural-language risk talk ("a 30% chance of overrun"), and is a single dimensionless number per Scenario. The percentile-rank form (P67) carries the same information but is harder to read aloud and gives no useful precision past two significant figures. *Expected overage* is a strictly better metric for decision theory but requires the reader to internalise a new unit (PM of expected overrun) that the rest of the table does not use; it could be added later as an additive column without re-opening this ADR. The fixed-percentile gap is dimensionally inconsistent with the percentile rows immediately above it (PM vs probability).

We chose to compute the exceedance probability via a **single binary search** over the sorted `Float64Array` rather than a linear scan. The binary search finds the first index where `sorted[idx] > capacity` in `O(log n)`, and `pExceed = (n − idx) / n` follows directly (`computeStats`, `index.html:2053-2058`). The linear scan is `O(n)` and would be invisible at the default iteration count, but the binary-search idiom is reused for per-marker `P(effort > value)` rows ([feature 0017](../../backtracked-features.md#0017), `computePExceed`, `index.html:2996`) and a per-team table can have ten or more markers active at once. Centralising on the binary form means a marker-heavy team table is `O(markers · log n)` instead of `O(markers · n)`. Both implementations rely on the sorted-`Float64Array` contract owned by [ADR-0006](./0006-monte-carlo-with-bootstrapped-sizes.md); this ADR does not add a new invariant.

We chose to render percentiles via **fractional-index lookup** — `sorted[min(n − 1, floor(p · n))]` — rather than the linearly-interpolated estimator used by NumPy's default (`linear` / type 7) or R's `quantile(type = 7)`. The simulator's `n` is at least 1,000 in practice and typically 10,000+; at that resolution the interpolated and non-interpolated values differ by less than the table's displayed decimal place, and the non-interpolated form has the desirable property that *every reported percentile is an actual realised **Iteration*** — the reader can be told "the P75 you see was the total effort of one specific Monte Carlo draw" without lying. A future move to a Hyndman & Fan type-7 estimator would require revisiting this ADR and adding a numerical-equivalence pin (which currently does not exist because there is no automated test harness — [ADR-0001](./0001-single-file-html-app.md)).

This decision pairs with [ADR-0006](./0006-monte-carlo-with-bootstrapped-sizes.md) (the sorted-distribution shape this layer consumes), [ADR-0010](./0010-three-scenario-moscow-forecasting.md) (the three-Scenario shape this table mirrors as three coloured columns), [ADR-0011](./0011-overlapping-histograms-shared-bins.md) (the chart whose distributions this table tabulates), and [ADR-0013](./0013-three-tier-risk-colouring.md) (the colour-tier semantics that turn the exceedance number into a verdict). The implementation lives in `computeStats` (`index.html:2048`) and `computePExceed` (`index.html:2996`); the table rendering belongs to [feature 0007](../plans/0007-org-level-summary-statistics-table.md).

A future revision could expose the percentile set or the percentile estimator as power-user knobs, or could add an *expected overage* column alongside the exceedance probability. The first two would re-open this ADR; the third would be additive and would not.
