# Monte Carlo simulation with bootstrapped historical sizes

Forecast effort by running a Monte Carlo: for each **Iteration**, sample the count of **Epics** per **Initiative** from `Poisson(λ)`, then sample each Epic's effort by drawing a **T-shirt size** uniformly with replacement from the historical-quarter's observed sizes ("bootstrap") and converting that label to person-months via the size-keyed lognormal. The output is the empirical distribution of total effort across many independent **Iterations**, not a point estimate.

We chose Monte Carlo over a closed-form analytic answer because the quantities users actually care about — *the probability that effort exceeds capacity*, the P75, the P90 — are tail statistics of a compound distribution (Poisson-sum of lognormals), and that compound has no clean closed form. Sampling is also much easier to explain to a non-statistician stakeholder than the convolution would be, which matters for a tool whose primary audience is product and engineering leads, not statisticians. The cost is wall-clock time and an iteration-count knob the user has to think about; we mitigate that by defaulting to 10,000 iterations (fast enough for an interactive Run, stable enough at P90 / P(exceed)) and exposing the knob in the sidebar.

We chose to *bootstrap* the t-shirt sizes from the historical quarter rather than fit a parametric multinomial because the observed mix is what the user is forecasting against, and any parametric fit would either need recalibration each quarter or be a Bayesian posterior that the user could not easily reason about. Bootstrapping also degrades gracefully: with a small historical sample, the simulator's output variance honestly reflects that the input data is thin, rather than being smoothed by a model. The trade-off is that an empty or extremely small historical pool produces a brittle forecast — which is a feature, not a bug, since the alternative would be silently fabricating data.
